# COLORS OF LIFE — Summary Map

*Written after reading every file, every line, in order.*
*For the author. By the work.*

---

## What This Document Is

This is not a plot synopsis.

A plot synopsis tells you what happened.

This map tells you what is *alive* in this story — and what still needs to breathe.

It traces the chronological and logical sequence of everything written,
everything planned, and the invisible thread running through all of it.

Indian readers do not read to be informed.
They read to *remember something they already know*.

This story is built for exactly that.

---

## The Author — Who Is Writing This

**Biswa Ranjan Panigrahi.**

A doctor. A quiet observer. Someone who notices that Vikram never left tea unfinished — and understands that a full, cold steel tumbler tells you more about a man's state of mind than ten sentences of description ever could.

His writing does not decorate. It notices.
It does not preach. It waits for the reader to feel, and only then lets the thought arrive.

His natural signature moves, now identified and mapped:

- **Short power lines** that land like a quiet slap: *"Just enough to hurt."*
- **Action reveals character** before any narrator explains it: removing both slippers so the old man doesn't look odd.
- **Vulnerability without words**: eyes go wet for one second — then he picks up the scalpel and checks its edge against the light. The scene moves. No dialogue needed, no lingering.
- **Silence as devastation**: Vikram walks out of the cabin without saying a single word.
- **The crowd as one collective character**: fifteen ordinary people forming a blood donor queue, none of them asked, none of them heroic.
- **Objects over speeches**: A full, cold tea tumbler. A half-underlined pathology textbook. A cracked brown leatherette chair listing two degrees left.
- **Philosophy arrived at through experience** — never delivered in advance.
- **Controlled chaos**: several unconnected things happening simultaneously until they turn out to be the same story.

This is the author. Every word in this project serves him — or it doesn't belong.

## The Story — One Line

A forgotten billionaire lives as an unknown old man.
An old friend, now a doctor, stumbles upon him in a hospital corridor.
What follows is not a biography.
It is a slow, honest question:
**Who defines a life as complete?**

---

## The Frame — Where We Are

**Year of narration: 2027.**
Dr. Adarsh Tripathy, approximately 50 years old, writes from memory.
Both Vikram and Adarsh were born around 1976/1977 — college batchmates, same generation.
In 2027, Adarsh looks like a mid-aged doctor. Nothing about him says old.
Vikram looks far older than he is — lean, thin, white-bearded, saint-like from years of living with nothing.
That contrast is the entire visual punch of the hospital opening.
A man of fifty who looks seventy. Not from age. From choice.

**The hospital incident: 2018.**
An old man, no known name, no address, no family, brought in after a road accident.
An NGO girl carries him. She is bleeding herself and does not notice.
The admission form reads: *Budha Baba.* Age: approximately 70. Relation with attendant: *NGO Volunteer.*

She had first written *Human.*
The receptionist told her to write something proper.
She crossed it out.

That crossing-out is the novel's first emotional statement.

## Chronological Map — Life of Vikram Ahuja

### Phase 0 — Before The Story Begins (Before 1977)

Binay Ahuja builds a business. A liquor and malt empire. North India presence. No southern roots yet.
His wife dies young. Vikram never knows her.
This absence — never dramatized, never explained — is the first wound the novel carries silently.

### Phase 1 — THE WHITE APRON (1995–1997, Medical College)

**Color:** Beginning. Innocence. Friendship. Identity before burden.
**Object:** White medical apron. Blue thread stitching. A mother's name sewn in.

**What is written (Parts 1, 2, 3 — all completed):**

Adarsh and Vikram meet in the anatomy dissection hall.
The apron — Adarsh's mother had stitched his name on the pocket.
Vikram sees it first, introduces himself as *Vicky*, and shakes hands.

*"My mom would have done the same. But she is no more. Lost her. Never seen. Anyway manageable."*

Eyes wet for one second. Then he picks up the scalpel and checks its edge against the light.

That is Vikram. In ten words. The whole character is there.

Ragging outside the anatomy hall follows. Seniors mock "Vicky" —
*"That's a dog's name, yaar. My cousin's Pomeranian is called Vicky."*
His full name emerges: Vikram Ahuja. Business family. Nike shoes.
Uncomfortable not because of the seniors, but because of the comparison with Adarsh's rubber slippers.

Tea at the wooden bench outside the canteen.
First silence of friendship.
Neither of them knew this would last thirty years.
Neither of them knew one of them would one day stand at a hospital corridor and struggle to believe the old man in the ICU was the same person.

**The hospital opening (2018) — how it actually begins:**

*"Please... side... please... give a little side..."*

No hero entry. No introduction. The hospital is already alive before we arrive.
A stretcher. An NGO girl, bleeding elbow, dust still on her palms.
One rubber slipper still on the old man's foot — the other lost somewhere on the road.
She stops. Bends down. Removes the remaining slipper. Places both carefully under the stretcher.
Ward boy: *"You know him?"*
She: *"No."*
*"Then why..."*
She: *"It doesn't look nice. Wearing only one slipper."*

The blood donation scene follows — her quiet revelation of AIDS in a narrow blood bank room.
*"I asked to give blood. I never said I could."*
The same people who stood shoulder to shoulder with her moments earlier now make a little space around her.
*Not much. Just enough to hurt.*

Then fifteen people step forward to donate blood. Flower seller. Postman. Auto driver. Security guard. Tea seller. Tailor.
The lab technician counts the queue.
*"For the first time... I have more donors than blood bags."*

Dr. Adarsh Tripathy enters. Young-looking white coat. Hears the name on the hospital register.
His hand stops mid-air over the attendance sheet.
He walks to the ICU door. Reads the case file.
DOB: 07-07-1977. Father: Binay Ahuja.
His hand trembles.
He says, quietly, what the whole novel really is:
*"I knew someone named Vikram Ahuja. But that Vikram would never become Budha Baba."*

He goes to the canteen. Holds a glass of tea. Remembers 1995.
Returns at night. The crowd has grown — flowers, fruits, a child's hand-drawn greeting card.
He meets Ananya — the NGO girl — and learns:
*"Three years ago I tried to end my life. He visited every day until I walked out alive. Today I returned the favor."*

At 11:30 PM, the old man's eyes open. First word: *"Ananya?"*

Adarsh cannot sleep that night.
The last line of Part 3 is this novel in a sentence:
*"The distance between a college laboratory and a hospital ICU is never just years. Sometimes it's an entire lifetime."*

**Status: 3 parts written. 6 more planned (Parts 4–9, hospital + college continuation).**
Part 3 is flagged in the skeleton as too essay-like in places — needs more Biswa rhythm.

### Phase 2 — THE BLUE COLLAR (1996–2007, Forced Into Business)

**Color:** Work. Responsibility. Duty over dreams.
**Object:** Dissection kit left on a shelf. A pathology textbook, half-underlined. A black rotary-dial phone chained to a hostel wall.

**What is written (Parts 1–6 — all completed):**

**Part 1 — Hostel. Winter 1996/97. Second year MBBS, six months in.**

Adarsh knocks on Vikram's door. The room is empty. Tea in the steel tumbler — *full, untouched, cold.*
Vikram never left tea unfinished. Not once in six months.
That cold full tumbler is how Adarsh knows something is wrong before he even finds Vikram.

He finds him at the hostel's one black rotary-dial phone, chained to the wall, listening.
*"When?"* *"How bad?"*

His father. Liver. *Decompensated.* A word Vikram makes the doctor repeat twice.

He packs. Two shirts. His hand touches the dissection kit — carefully oiled since first year.
He almost packs it. He doesn't. He puts it back exactly where it was.
*Like leaving a place for something to return to.*

He looks at the pathology textbook on the desk. Half-underlined. He doesn't pack that either.

Adarsh walks him to the gate. Night. A stray dog follows them halfway, then loses interest.
*"Papa always wanted a doctor in the family. 'My son will wear the white coat.' Like it was already done. I don't think that's happening now."*
*"Don't decide anything tonight."*
*"I'm not deciding. I'm telling you what I already know."*

Adarsh goes back to the room after the rickshaw pulls away.
He picks up the pathology textbook. Closes it. Keeps it in the cupboard.
Waiting for a return that would take twenty-two years.
Vikram never came back.

**Part 2 — The hospital. The modest takeover.**

Father propped on two flat pillows, jaundiced, saline taped into the back of his hand.
*"Can you stay some weeks. Just to sign what Pradhan-babu tells you to sign."*
*"Some weeks," Vikram repeated.*
*It did not feel like all he was asking.*

Pradhan-babu: sixty-one years old. Brown cardigan every day, regardless of weather.
Typewriter that jams on the letter M.
Vikram signs his name — *wrong-sized, a schoolboy's hand trying to look official.*
He greets the factory supervisor with *"Sir"* by reflex. The supervisor looks behind him to see who Vikram is addressing.
Nobody is there.
Pradhan-babu, without looking up: *"You're Ahuja Sahib now. People say sir to you."*

Vikram felt his ears go warm — the exact same warmth from a different room, a different year, a stitched name on an apron.

Some weeks became six weeks. Six weeks became a semester he didn't return for.
Father came home thinner, slower, but home.
Neither of them mentioned the white coat again.
*Some things are easier to leave unfinished than to formally end.*

**Part 3 — The decade compressed. Business. Forbes. Ira.**

Business growth: Forbes list, three years running.
Misspelling Ahuja as Ahluja in at least one edition —
annoyed Pradhan-babu more than it annoyed Vikram.

Liquor policy crisis: Eleven months, two industry associations, one press conference.
He didn't celebrate. He read the fine print twice,
the way some men reread a letter from someone they miss.

Ira Sen: A journalist from a mid-size paper.
*"Everyone talks about the license survival. Nobody's asking what it cost you personally."*
*"Is that an interview question?"*
*"No. That one's for me."*

He liked that she said it.

Their relationship — months of unhurried evenings.
Her thumb along his collarbone while he was still carrying board pressure he hadn't managed to leave at the office.
He gave her a supplementary card. The kind of gesture rich men make when they want love to feel like less of a transaction.

Then: Durga Puja. She says she's leaving for Delhi. Tuesday.

Wednesday: a bank alert on his phone. CCD near the press building. Salt Lake. Eleven kilometres from the airport.

He decides to surprise her. He has a car for her —
cream-colored, ribbon still in the boot, hadn't decided yet whether that was too much or exactly enough.
He drives it there himself.

Her cabin. Second floor. Door ajar. He pushes it open.

*Her blouse, unbuttoned wrong, hung off one shoulder.*
*A man's belt on the floor. Rajeev. Junior sub-editor.*

He didn't shout. He didn't ask a single question he already had every answer to.
He stepped back.
Closed the door as quietly as he had opened it.

Outside, the cream-colored car sat with the ribbon still in the boot.
He picked up a stone from the flowerbed.
Brought it down once, deliberately, on the windshield.
*Not in fury. Like arithmetic — a man solving one final sum before closing the book.*

Ira came down forty minutes later.
The security guard: *"Vikram sir came. Brought this car for you. Left it there. Then left in anger."*
Rajeev: *"Did he see us?"*
Nobody answered.

He never spoke her name again.
The card was cancelled the next morning — a five-minute phone call, no note, no message.
*Some silences are just silence. Others are the loudest thing a man ever says.*

**Part 4 — Terminal diagnosis. The real board meeting. 2007.**

The doctor's voice doesn't leave any doors open.
*"Weeks. Possibly a few months, if he's fortunate and careful."*

The boardroom. Twelve men. Binay at the head — thinner than the frailty of ten quiet years should account for.
Sinha: *"We need younger perspective."*
Reddy: *"South India needs experience. Not a fresh graduate's confidence."*

Vikram thinks of a hostel corridor phone, a pathology book, a factory supervisor calling him "sir" by mistake.

*"Nothing. I know nothing about it yet."*
Reddy almost smiles. Confirms something.
*"But I know what it costs to become known somewhere new. I've paid that bill once already."*

Reddy: *"Still. A kid taking on a grown man's risk."*
Binay, one hand raised: *"Meeting adjourned."*
His voice cost him something to produce.

**Part 5 — The window promise.**

Vikram stands at the boardroom window after everyone has left.
Binay comes back.

*"They don't believe you can do it."*
*"I've been doing it for ten years and nobody in that room noticed."*
*"That's the wrong complaint. The question was never whether they noticed. Will you do it again?"*

In the window's reflection, just for a blink, the face looking back is thinner. Younger. A hostel phone still warm against one ear.

The promise he makes is not to Reddy. Not even to Binay.
It is to a nineteen-year-old who once left a pathology textbook open mid-sentence and never got to finish it.
*I'll finish something, for you. Not that. But something.*

*"Tell them yes. South India. All of it."*

**Part 6 — Adarsh narrates (2027 retrospective).**

*"I didn't know any of this when it happened. I pieced it together the way you piece together most of a friend's life you weren't present for."*

What he knows because Vikram told him directly, once, sitting outside the ICU:
*"I never wanted the business, Adarsh. Twice life decided for me. Not once."*
*"1996. Then 2007. My father's liver, both times."*
*"He didn't run either time."*

### Phase 3 — THE BROWN CHAIR (2007–2012, South India — Leadership By Burden)

**Color:** Leadership. Authority. The weight of decisions.
**Object:** A cracked brown leatherette chair, one wheel that lists two degrees left.

**What is written (Parts 1–9 — all completed):**

**Parts 1–2 — The Andhra Pradesh farmhouse. The Aghori.**

The company has no office in this state yet.
Ramanna keeps the farmhouse — dhoti pressed into permanent wrinkles,
calls every guest "sir" in a tone that sounds like a question.

*"Board members usually don't come themselves. They send juniors."*
*"I'm the junior,"* Vikram says.

The Aghori at the jungle's edge. Fire at midnight. Ash, lifted and let fall.
Ramanna warned him: *"Don't go ask him. He'll tell you. That's the trouble with him."*
Vikram doesn't take the advice.

Three nights of conversation:
*"Your father's company. Whose is it now?"*
*"Everything you're building — this will be. Doesn't mean don't build it."*
*"A man who expects the tea to go cold still drinks it while it's hot. Only a fool waits for it to stay warm forever."*

**Part 3 — Lakshmi's wedding.**

Ramanna's daughter. Bamboo pandal, banana stems, mango leaves breathing in a line.
Someone puts a plate in his hands before he's finished taking his shoes off.
Four different aunts feed him. Nobody wants a licence signed or a policy explained.

He calls his PA from the veranda. The wedding band still going behind him.
*"Are you well? You sound different."*
*"I've been eating a lot of sambar,"* Vikram says.

He meant it sincerely, and nobody quite understood that.

**Parts 4–5 — Minister Satyanarayana. The land question.**

Small man behind a very large desk. Liquor is a political liability in this state.
*"Everything about the moral question changes based on whose name is on the license."*

The Aghori, at departure:
*"Why this? Liquor. Not medicine, not schools."*
Vikram doesn't give the boardroom answer.
*"It's not mine to choose. It was my father's. I never wanted it. Twice life put me in it anyway."*
*"And the liquor part — does that trouble you?"*
*"Yes."* No softening.

The Aghori smiles. Gives Vikram a folded paper — a yoga posture, one word, underlined.
Vikram carries it in his inside pocket without quite noticing.

**Part 6 — The mysterious reversal.**

Eleven days later: the MLA clears the land allocation. Double the acreage. No conditions.
Nobody knows why.

*"You don't sound like it's good news."*
*"I'm glad. I just don't feel like I need to do anything about being glad."*

That evening, he sits for ten minutes with the folded paper.
*He felt like a man who had stopped needing the outcome to arrive on time in order to keep doing the work anyway.*

Somewhere past the hotel window, a fire was probably still burning at the edge of a coffee plantation.
He didn't call to tell the Aghori.
*Some things weren't owed an explanation. Not even a grateful one.*

**Part 7 — The chair itself.**

Two rooms above a spare-parts godown. Brown leatherette, cracked along both arms.
Suresh tried twice to replace it.
*"This one works."*
*"It's falling apart, sir."*
*"It hasn't fallen apart yet."*

He sits in it the night eleven workers are unpaid.
Past midnight, numbers twice, releases partial payment from a different account.
Workers get paid before they arrive to ask.

*"Every time I sit in something comfortable, I start making comfortable decisions. This one reminds me every few minutes that it could give way. Keeps me honest about what's at stake when I sign something."*

Reddy, hearing this, looks at him for a long moment.
Revising an old opinion.

**Part 8 — Reddy reports to Binay. Numbers and a private restlessness.**

Seven hundred crore when Vikram took over. Now fourteen hundred.
South alone: six hundred crore, manufactured and exported worldwide.
Plans for ten thousand crore within five years.

*"You were the one who called him a kid."*
*"I was. I'll say it again if I'm ever wrong about someone else. I was wrong about this one."*

Reddy rests a hand on Vikram's shoulder as he leaves.
Not a handshake, not a speech. Just a hand, there and gone.
Something loosens in Vikram's chest.

Then the warmth fades. Faster than expected.
*Duty discharged, not dream fulfilled.*
*What would I have built, if I'd been building for myself instead of for him.*

**Part 9 — Rohan's arrest. The seed of envy.**

Rohan took a site-engineer job without telling anyone whose brother he was.
Refused to certify substandard concrete work.
Contractor retaliated. Police came to his house.

Vikram arrives with a lawyer. The same inspector who ignored Rohan for hours
suddenly offers tea and pulls out chairs.
Rohan watches all of it from the bench. Swollen lip.

*"You'd have done the same."*
*"Would I. I don't have a lawyer on retainer who makes inspectors pull out chairs, Vikram."*

He doesn't say what he saw. But something shifts in his face.
*Something Vikram noticed and couldn't yet name,
and Rohan spent the rest of the drive making sure his brother never saw it clearly enough to try.*

## The Chapters Ahead — Planned But Not Yet Written

### Phase 4 — THE RED TEA (2012–2015)

**Color:** Human warmth. Simplicity. The surrogate family.
**Object:** Mismatched clay cups. A three-legged wooden horse. The cheap red tea served daily at the NGO.

- **The Transition**: Binay Ahuja dies first. Rohan creates a public scene, accusing Vikram of dominating everything. Vikram, who never wanted the corporate life, signs over the entire 1400-crore empire and the house to Rohan.
- **The New Home**: Vikram walks out of his family home with a single bag and joins the Odisha AIDS shelter/NGO permanently. The patients and children there become his surrogate family.
- **Sarita**: A close friend of Vikram's, a widow of the same age (~35). She also lives with HIV.
- **Ananya**: A young teenager (14-15 years old) living at the NGO. Vikram befriends her, helping her set up her three-legged wooden horse and promising to teach her the "pink chalk" school words.
- **The Red Tea**: The low-cost tea served at the NGO. Having walked away from Nandyal and the boardrooms, this cheap roadside/NGO tea smells and tastes better to Vikram because he is actually free.

### Phase 5 — THE YELLOW ENVELOPE (2015–2018)

**Color:** Truth. Revelation. Identity crisis. The turning point in Kolkata.

1. **Move to Kolkata**: After their time in Odisha, Vikram and Sarita decide to shift the operation to Kolkata. They set up the NGO in an abandoned ancestral mansion belonging to Sarita's late husband's family.
2. **The Kolkata Rebuild**: The Kolkata NGO grows into **Punarjeevan** — a global AIDS rehabilitation organization.
3. **The Yellow Envelope Mystery (locked — must not be revealed early)**:
   - A letter arrives for Vikram — appears to be from his Odisha-era ex-girlfriend, Ira Sen, seeking help for her son.
   - She claims her assistant/colleague Rajeev forced her to convert, abused her, and abandoned her with a child. She asks Vikram to train her son, Rehman, as a way to ease her guilt.
   - A young man named **Rehman** arrives the next day to work under Vikram.
4. **The Hallucination Truth (revealed only at the end of Chapter 6)**:
   - Rehman is a hallucination. The letter was actually about a property issue. Rehman is Vikram's own sub-conscious doubt about giving up his empire, given a face and voice.
   - The real Ira Sen is alive, well, and happily married to Aftab in Odisha. She has no son. Use of Rehman's character should make him read as completely real until the climax.

### Phase 6 — THE GREEN LAWN (2018–2023)

**Color:** Time. Memory. Growth beyond profit. Same park bench, different years.

Vikram's conversations with Rehman — philosophical, real-feeling throughout this chapter.
The debate: Vikram's "empty hands" vs. Rehman's "money circulated is money doing good."
Together they launch **Ummed** — medical kits, agarbathi, sanitary pads.
Rehman names it after his "mother." First flicker of unease in Vikram.

At Sarita's return party — Vikram still center of attention despite Rehman running everything.
**The drunk terrace confrontation:** Rehman accuses Vikram of unfair legacy.
*"A candle in front of daylight."*
Vikram's response: transfers CEO power to Rehman. Leaves the city.

Rehman vanishes. A phone call — shame, refusal to meet. Then nothing.

**The crack — at a press meet:**
The real Ira Sen appears. She is well. Happily married to Aftab.
Vikram asks about her son.
*She says she never had a son.*

Rehman's phone number: does not exist.
His "photo" on the phone: it is Vikram's own old college photo.
The lawyer: no employee named Rehman was ever on record.

The transfer was never real. Everything still belongs to Vikram.

### Phase 7 — COLORLESS TEARS (2023–2027)

**Color:** Loss of identity. Pure emotion. Life beyond labels.

Vikram leaves the service empire. Small room near a school. Teaching children.
Society remembers "Budha Baba." Nobody remembers Vikram Ahuja.

**The loop closes:**
A call comes — another AIDS patient at the NGO is attempting suicide.
Vikram goes immediately. Talks her down.
Afterward, an MLA offers a lift. Vikram insists on walking alone.
He is hit in a bike-strike accident. This is the accident that opens Chapter 1.

**Vikram does not recover. He dies before Adarsh can meet him.**

The climax: a psychiatrist's office.
Adarsh and the psychiatrist who evaluated Vikram.
She walks him through what Vikram's life and mind held.

*"His smiles were transparent. His tears were colorless."*

The question the novel leaves open:
**"Who is the loser? Vikram? Or we?"**

## The Characters — Who They Are

**Vikram Ahuja (Vicky / Budha Baba)**
Never a hero. Never a saint. A man called to phases of life he didn't choose, fulfilled each one completely, and left each one voluntarily — not to escape, but because completion is its own destination. His losses: a mother never known. A stethoscope never worn. A love destroyed in absolute silence. His inheritance: a business, a responsibility, a question he never stopped asking quietly.

**Dr. Adarsh Tripathy**
The reader's representative inside the story. Quiet, observant, emotionally honest.
He notices what other people miss — the cold tea, the scalpel checked against light.
His job is not to be impressive. His job is to feel what the reader would feel, slightly ahead of them, and let them catch up.

**Binay Ahuja**
Strongest father character so far. Intelligent. Clear-eyed about his own decline.
Asks for "some weeks" — knowing it is not some weeks.
*"Confident about being unsure. That's the job."*
He dies having watched his son succeed. Whether that was enough — the novel doesn't say.
It simply shows the window. The city lights. Both men standing together, saying nothing else.

**Reddy**
Not a villain. His logic is sound. He applies the wrong equation to Vikram — assumes the man who quietly built South India for ten years is an untested kid. He is wrong and admits it.
*"I'll say it again if I'm ever wrong about someone else. I was wrong about this one."*
One hand on Vikram's shoulder. There and gone.

**Ananya**
The girl who carries Budha Baba into the hospital. She is bleeding herself. She doesn't notice.
She wrote *Human* on the admission form. They made her cross it out.
She is the novel's opening emotional statement — a person saving another life while quietly living with her own slow emergency.

**Rehman**
Must feel completely real throughout Chapters 5 and 6.
His philosophy is not wrong — money needs to circulate, power enables good at scale.
His grief on the terrace is genuinely human.
He is Vikram's own doubt, given a face and a voice.
The twist, when it comes, must devastate precisely because readers loved him.

**Ira Sen**
She asked the question nobody else asked: *"What did it cost you personally to fight it?"*
He liked that she said it. That was the entire attraction in one sentence.
The betrayal — discovered in absolute silence — is the wound that turns Vikram inward for years.
She reappears later: healthy, normal, happy. She sold the wrecked car and built something real from the money.
A detail that says everything about both of them.

**Rohan**
He wanted to carry his own name. He took a job without telling anyone whose brother he was.
He refused to certify bad concrete. He was arrested, beaten, then watched a lawyer make inspectors pull out chairs.
He doesn't call this jealousy. He doesn't name it at all.
But it lives in the flat "thank you" in the car, and in the look he makes sure his brother never quite sees clearly.

## The Indian Reader — What This Story Gives Them

Indian readers — especially those who watched parents build something from nothing, or sacrifice a profession for a family crisis, or silently carry a grief they never dramatized — will recognize Vikram the moment the cold tea tumbler appears.

They have watched a father keep working at a job he never wanted.
They have felt the particular flatness of a duty discharged rather than a dream fulfilled.
They know what it means to leave a pathology textbook half-underlined.

This story does not give them a hero to worship.
It gives them a mirror.

When the question finally arrives — *Who is the loser? Vikram? Or we?* —
Indian readers will not answer it intellectually.
They will answer it personally.

That is the only thing a great novel ever really does.
