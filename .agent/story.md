# Novel

Title

Colors of Life

Genre

Literary Fiction

Psychological Drama

Mystery

Life Journey

Philosophical Fiction

Primary Theme

Every phase of life has its own colour.

People spend their entire lives chasing one colour,

without realizing life was beautiful because every colour existed.

---

# One Line

A forgotten billionaire living as an unknown old man slowly forces an old friend to question success, memory, identity and reality itself.

---

# Central Question

Who was Vikram Ahuja?

The novel should not answer this immediately.

Readers should slowly assemble the answer.

# Locked Story Facts

Vikram and Adarsh first meet during engineering college.

The chemistry apron establishes their first connection.

Mother's stitched name becomes the memorable detail.

Ragging naturally reveals Vikram's full name.

Father's terminal illness forces Vikram into business.

Reddy questions Vikram's leadership.

Sinha supports him.

Vikram accepts the challenge.

He transforms the southern business.

He later transfers his inherited business.

He builds a much larger NGO.

The NGO eventually grows beyond the inherited empire.

He leaves that as well.

He finally lives a simple anonymous life.

Society remembers "Budha Baba."

Very few remember Vikram Ahuja.

# Narrative Principles

Readers should always know less than they think.

Mysteries should naturally accumulate.

Answers should feel inevitable rather than surprising.

No twist should exist merely for shock.

Every revelation must emotionally reframe previous chapters.

---

# Forbidden

Do not introduce supernatural explanations.

Do not introduce time travel.

Do not introduce multiple universes.

The story must always remain psychologically believable.

Coincidences may begin stories.

They must never resolve them.

---

# Ending Philosophy

The ending should answer emotional questions before factual ones.

Readers should continue thinking after finishing the novel.

The final pages should feel peaceful rather than dramatic.

---

# Locked Future Direction

The novel ultimately becomes less about Vikram,

and more about the reader questioning:

Who defines success?

What remains after achievement?

How much of our identity survives memory?

Who are we when everything we built no longer belongs to us?

These questions should emerge naturally.

Never through speeches.
