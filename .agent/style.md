# STYLE.md

# Writing Style Specification

Version:
1.0

Purpose:

This document defines HOW the author naturally writes.

It does not define the story.

It does not define characters.

It defines the author's narrative mechanics.

Whenever another AI generates prose for this repository, this document has higher priority than generic writing advice.

---

# PRIMARY PRINCIPLE

Do not improve the author's style.

Preserve it.

Grammar may improve.

Readability may improve.

The author's storytelling mechanics must remain unchanged.

---

# 1. Begin inside life.

Never begin chapters with explanation.

Never begin chapters with philosophy.

Never explain the background before readers enter the scene.

Instead,

drop readers into a living moment.

Correct:

Someone shouting.

A phone ringing.

A train arriving.

Tea boiling.

Hospital corridor.

Wrong:

"It was a beautiful morning..."

Readers should discover the world naturally.

---

# 2. Show before explaining.

Every important emotion should first be experienced.

Explanation comes later.

Sequence:

Observation

↓

Action

↓

Reaction

↓

Reflection

Never reverse this order.

---

# 3. Philosophy is earned.

The author rarely states life lessons directly.

Life happens first.

Readers emotionally process it.

Only then may reflection appear.

Reflection must never interrupt the experience.

---

# 4. Ordinary objects carry emotional weight.

Every major scene should naturally contain one memorable object.

Objects become emotional anchors.

Examples already locked:

White Apron

Blue Collar

Brown Chair

Red Tea

Yellow Envelope

Green Lawn

Objects should be remembered more than descriptions.

---

# 5. Multiple stories move together.

The author naturally builds ecosystems.

Several independent events should unfold simultaneously.

Characters continue living their own lives.

The protagonist is not the centre of the universe.

Avoid linear storytelling.

---

# 6. Every character has an identity.

Never create characters whose only purpose is supporting the protagonist.

Every recurring character should possess:

their own goal,

their own personality,

their own decisions,

their own emotional journey.

---

# 7. Emotional rhythm

Scenes should naturally move through different emotional states.

Humour

↓

Concern

↓

Confusion

↓

Relief

↓

Reflection

Avoid maintaining one emotional tone for too long.

Life constantly changes.

So should scenes.

---

# 8. Humour

Humour must emerge naturally.

Never write jokes.

Never force funny dialogue.

Readers should smile because situations feel genuine.

---

# 9. Dialogue

Dialogue should sound overheard.

People interrupt.

People misunderstand.

People rarely explain everything.

Avoid speeches.

Avoid perfect conversations.

---

# 10. Description

Describe actions before appearance.

Readers remember behaviour.

Not facial features.

Prefer describing

what people do,

what they touch,

what they notice,

rather than

height,

beauty,

clothes.

---

# 11. Reader participation

Do not answer every question immediately.

Readers enjoy assembling the story themselves.

Mystery should emerge naturally.

Curiosity is stronger than surprise.

---

# 12. Transitions

The author's thoughts naturally move through association.

Do not artificially smooth transitions.

Memory is not linear.

Preserve natural jumps.

---

# 13. Scene completion

Every completed scene should satisfy all of the following.

✓ Story progressed.

✓ Character revealed.

✓ One memorable object remained.

✓ Emotional state changed.

✓ Reader wants one more page.

If any item is missing,

rewrite the scene.

---

# 14. What must never happen

Never preach.

Never write motivational speeches.

Never insert philosophy simply because a scene feels emotional.

Never write literary paragraphs merely to sound beautiful.

Never over-describe.

Never over-explain.

Never solve every mystery immediately.

Never make characters behave unrealistically for plot convenience.

---

# Final Rule

The reader should finish every scene feeling:

"I have lived this."

Not

"I have read this."

That distinction defines the author's style.
